The Biological Background

Immunity is the body's ability to fight diseases or infections caused by internal or external factors or organisms[cite: 43]. The body does this with the help of neutralizing and non-neutralizing antibodies[cite: 44]. These antibodies develop in response to infection and vaccination, therefore providing protection (dePillis et al., 2023)[cite: 45].

During the 2020 COVID-19 outbreak, vaccines were developed by companies such as Moderna, Pfizer, and Johnson & Johnson[cite: 49]. Specifically, Pfizer and Moderna vaccines are mRNA vaccines because, instead of containing weakened viruses like most vaccines, they introduce a synthetic messenger RNA strand to the individual’s body[cite: 50].

💉 ➔ 🧬 ➔ 🛡️

Visual Pathway: The mRNA vaccines are made from a recombinant SARS-CoV-2 spike protein[cite: 52]. The body identifies this as foreign and produces neutralizing antibodies (NAb) that bind to or attack it[cite: 53].

The neutralizing antibodies produced inhibit the binding of ACE2 to the spike protein of SARS-CoV-2[cite: 54]. Hence, there is a need to study how the concentrations of these NAbs affect the body’s response to the virus over time[cite: 55].
